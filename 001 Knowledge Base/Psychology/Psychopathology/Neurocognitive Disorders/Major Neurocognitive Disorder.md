# Major Neurocognitive Disorder (Dementia)

An acquired syndrome of cognitive decline sufficient to interfere with independence in daily activities.

## Diagnosis (DSM-5 Criteria)
- **Code**: Varies by etiology (e.g., 331.0 for Alzheimer's type).
- **Essential Features**:
    - **Significant cognitive decline** from a previous level of performance in one or more cognitive domains (complex attention, executive function, learning and memory, language, perceptual-motor, or social cognition).
    - **Evidence**:
        1.  Concern of the individual, a knowledgeable informant, or the clinician.
        2.  A substantial impairment in cognitive performance (preferably documented by standardized testing, typically >2 SD below norms).
    - **Interference with Independence**: The cognitive deficits interfere with independence in everyday activities (e.g., requiring assistance with paying bills, managing medications).
    - **Exclusion**: Not better explained by delirium or another mental disorder (e.g., Major Depression, Schizophrenia).
- **Specifiers**:
    - **Etiology**: Alzheimer’s disease, Frontotemporal lobar degeneration, Lewy body disease, Vascular disease, Traumatic brain injury, Substance/medication use, HIV infection, Prion disease, Parkinson’s disease, Huntington’s disease.
    - **Severity**: Mild (instrumental ADLs affected), Moderate (basic ADLs affected), Severe (fully dependent).

## Diagnosis (ICD-11 Criteria)
- **Name**: **Dementia** (Code 6D80-6D8Z)
- **Essential Features**:
    - **Acquired cognitive decline** from a previously attained level of functioning.
    - **Significant decline** in one or more cognitive domains (memory, language, visuospatial, executive).
    - **Functional Impairment**: Represents a decline in the individual's ability to perform daily activities and maintain autonomy.
- **Key Differences**:
    - **Terminology**: DSM-5 adopted "Major Neurocognitive Disorder" to reduce stigma and include younger populations (TBI, HIV) where "Dementia" implies aging. ICD-11 retains "Dementia" as the primary term but acknowledges it as a syndrome caused by various diseases.
    - **Thresholds**: Both define the boundary between Mild and Major/Dementia based on the **loss of independence** in daily functioning (ADLs).

## Clinical Context
- **Reversibility**: While most causes (Alzheimer's) are progressive and irreversible, some "reversible dementias" (vitamin B12 deficiency, hypothyroidism, normal pressure hydrocephalus) must be ruled out.
