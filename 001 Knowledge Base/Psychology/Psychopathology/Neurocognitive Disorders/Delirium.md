# Delirium

An acute, fluctuating disturbance in attention and awareness, typically initiated by a medical condition or substance.

## Diagnosis (DSM-5 Criteria)
- **Code**: 780.09 (R41.0)
- **Essential Features**:
    - **Disturbance in Attention**: Reduced ability to direct, focus, sustain, and shift attention.
    - **Disturbance in Awareness**: Reduced orientation to the environment.
    - **Acute Onset**: The disturbance develops over a short period (hours to a few days), represents a change from baseline, and **fluctuates** in severity during the day.
    - **Additional Cognitive Disturbance**: Deficits in memory, orientation, language, visuospatial ability, or perception.
    - **Direct Physiological Consequence**: Evidence that the disturbance is caused by a medical condition, substance intoxication/withdrawal, or toxin exposure.
    - **Exclusion**: Not better explained by a pre-existing neurocognitive disorder (though they often co-occur) and not occurring in the context of a severely reduced level of arousal (coma).

## Diagnosis (ICD-11 Criteria)
- **Code**: 6D70
- **Essential Features**:
    - **Disturbance of attention, orientation, and awareness**.
    - **Short period** of development (acute).
    - **Fluctuating** symptoms.
    - **Sleep-Wake Disturbance**: Explicitly mentions sleep-wake cycle disturbance (e.g., reversal, sundowning) as a common feature.
    - **Etiology**: Attributable to direct physiological effects of a medical condition or substance.
- **Key Differences**:
    - **Sleep-Wake**: ICD-11 highlights sleep-wake disturbances and psychomotor disturbances even more explicitly as core features, though they are also recognized features in DSM-5 text.

## Clinical Context
- **Medical Emergency**: Delirium is often a sign of acute medical instability (infection/sepsis, metabolic imbalance, organ failure) and requires immediate medical investigation.
- **Vulnerability**: The "frail brain" (e.g., existing dementia) is highly susceptible to delirium even from minor insults (e.g., UTI).
- **Subtypes**:
    - **Hyperactive**: Agitation, hallucinations (often alcohol withdrawal).
    - **Hypoactive**: Lethargy, quiet confusion (often missed).
    - **Mixed**: Fluctuating between the two.
