# Schizophrenia

Schizophrenia is a serious mental disorder in which people interpret reality abnormally. It may result in some combination of hallucinations, delusions, and extremely disordered thinking and behavior that impairs daily functioning.

## Diagnosis (DSM-5 Criteria)
- **Criterion A**: Two (or more) of the following, each present for a significant portion of time during a 1-month period (or less if successfully treated). At least one of these must be (1), (2), or (3):
    1. Delusions.
    2. Hallucinations.
    3. Disorganized speech (e.g., frequent derailment or incoherence).
    4. Grossly disorganized or catatonic behavior.
    5. Negative symptoms (i.e., diminished emotional expression or avolition).
- **Criterion B**: For a significant portion of the time since the onset of the disturbance, level of functioning in one or more major areas, such as work, interpersonal relations, or self-care, is markedly below the level achieved prior to the onset.
- **Criterion C**: Continuous signs of the disturbance persist for at least 6 months.

## Diagnosis (ICD-11 Criteria)
- **Code**: 6A20
- **Essential Features**:
    - Characterized by disturbances in multiple mental modalities, including thinking, perception, self-experience, cognition, volition, affect, and behavior.
    - At least **two** of the following symptoms must be present for most of the time for at least **1 month**:
        1.  **Persistent delusions** (e.g., grandiose, persecutory).
        2.  **Persistent hallucinations** (any modality).
        3.  **Disorganized thinking** (formal thought disorder).
        4.  **Experiences of influence, passivity, or control** (e.g., thought insertion).
        5.  Negative symptoms (e.g., affective flattening, avolition).
        6.  Grossly disorganized behavior.
        7.  Psychomotor disturbances (e.g., catatonia).
    - **Requirement**: At least one of the symptoms must be from the first four "Core" categories (Delusions, Hallucinations, Disorganized thinking, or Experiences of influence).
- **Key Differences from DSM-5**:
    - **Duration**: ICD-11 requires symptoms to persist for at least **1 month** (DSM-5 requires continuous signs for 6 months).
    - **Functional Impairment**: Not explicitly required as a diagnostic criterion in ICD-11 (though implied), whereas DSM-5 strictly lists marked functional decline as a criterion.


## Symptom Categories
- **Positive Symptoms**: Hallucinations (hearing voices), delusions, thought disorders.
- **Negative Symptoms**: Reduced ability to function normally, lack of emotion, loss of interest.
- **Cognitive Symptoms**: Problems with attention, memory, and executive function.

## Etiology and Links
- **Neurobiology**: The dopamine hypothesis is a longstanding theory.
- **Genetic Overlap**: Significant genetic overlap with [[Autism]] and other neurodevelopmental conditions.
