# Premature Ejaculation (Male Early Ejaculation)

A persistent pattern of ejaculation occurring before the individual wishes it, usually with minimal sexual stimulation.

## Diagnosis (DSM-5 Criteria)
- **Code**: 302.75
- **Essential Features**:
    - A persistent or recurrent pattern of ejaculation occurring during partnered sexual activity **within approximately 1 minute** following vaginal penetration and before the individual wishes it.
    - **Frequency**: Occurs on **almost all or all (75-100%)** occasions of sexual activity.
    - **Duration**: Persisted for approximately **6 months**.
    - **Distress**: Causes clinically significant distress in the individual.
    - **Severity Specifiers**:
        - Mild: Within 30 secs to 1 minute.
        - Moderate: Within 15-30 seconds.
        - Severe: Prior to sexual activity, at start, or within 15 seconds.

## Diagnosis (ICD-11 Criteria)
- **Name**: **Male Early Ejaculation** (Code 6C42)
- **Essential Features**:
    - Ejaculation occurs prior to or within a very short duration of the initiation of vaginal penetration (or other relevant sexual stimulation).
    - **No Control**: The individual has no or little perceived control over ejaculation.
    - **Duration**: At least **several months**.
    - **Distress**: Associated with clinically significant distress.
- **Key Differences**:
    - **Flexibility**: ICD-11 avoids the rigid "1 minute" cutoff found in DSM-5, preferring qualitative descriptions ("very short duration") and emphasizing the **lack of perceived control**.
    - **Subjective Experience**: ICD-11 distinguishes between the subjective experience of orgasm and the physical act of ejaculation (relevant for rare cases where they dissociate).

## Clinical Context
- **Most Common Dysfunction**: The most common sexual dysfunction in men under 40.
- **Primary vs. Secondary**:
    - **Primary (Lifelong)**: Present since first sexual experience.
    - **Secondary (Acquired)**: Develops after a period of normal functioning (often linked to ED, prostatitis, or thyroid issues).
- **Treatment**: Behavioral techniques (stop-start, squeeze) and SSRIs (off-label) are common.
