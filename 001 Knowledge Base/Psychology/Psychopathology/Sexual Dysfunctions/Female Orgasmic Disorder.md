# Female Orgasmic Disorder

A disorder characterized by persistent or recurrent delay, infrequent, or absent sensation of orgasm following a normal sexual excitement phase.

## Diagnosis (DSM-5 Criteria)
- **Code**: 302.73
- **Essential Features**:
    - Presence of either of the following on **almost all or all (75-100%)** occasions of sexual activity:
        1.  Marked delay in, marked infrequency of, or absence of orgasm.
        2.  Markedly reduced intensity of orgasmic sensations.
    - **Duration**: Symptoms have persisted for approximately **6 months**.
    - **Distress**: Causes clinically significant distress in the individual.
    - **Exclusion**: Not better explained by a nonsexual mental disorder, severe relationship distress (e.g., partner violence), or other medical/substance factors.
- **Specifiers**:
    - **Lifelong** vs. **Acquired**.
    - **Generalized** vs. **Situational**.
    - **Severity**: Mild, Moderate, Severe.

## Diagnosis (ICD-11 Criteria)
- **Name**: **Female Orgasmic Disorder** (Code 6C44)
- **Essential Features**:
    - Persistent or recurrent pattern of:
        - Delay or absence of orgasm.
        - Markedly diminished intensity of orgasmic sensation.
    - Occurs despite **adequate sexual stimulation** and **desire**.
    - **Duration**: Several months.
    - **Distress**: Clinically significant distress is a required feature.
- **Key Differences**:
    - ICD-11 applies "Orgasmic Dysfunction" to both men and women (Male Orgasmic Disorder exists as 6C43), whereas DSM-5 separates them.
    - ICD-11 emphasizes the context of "adequate sexual stimulation," acknowledging that inability to orgasm due to poor technique or stimulation is not a disorder.

## Clinical Context
- **Context is Key**: Diagnosis should not be made if the difficulty is due to inadequate stimulation (e.g., lack of clitoral stimulation for many women).
- **Secondary to Other Issues**: Often secondary to low desire, pain (dyspareunia), or relationship conflict.
