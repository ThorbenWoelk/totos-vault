# Intermittent Explosive Disorder (IED)

A disorder characterized by recurrent behavioral outbursts representing a failure to control aggressive impulses, which are grossly disproportionate to the provocation.

## Diagnosis (DSM-5 Criteria)
- **Code**: 312.34
- **Essential Features**:
    - Recurrent behavioral outbursts representing a failure to control aggressive impulses, manifested by either:
        1.  **Verbal aggression** (tantrums, arguments) or **physical aggression** toward property/animals/people (without damage/injury) occurring twice weekly on average for 3 months.
        2.  **Harmful Outbursts**: Three behavioral outbursts involving actual damage/destruction of property or physical injury to animals/people within a 12-month period.
    - **Proportionality**: The magnitude of aggressiveness is grossly out of proportion to the provocation.
    - **Impulsive**: Outbursts are not premeditated (i.e., they are impulsive/anger-based) and not committed to achieve a tangible objective (e.g., money, power).
    - **Distress/Impairment**: Causes marked distress, impairment, or financial/legal consequences.
    - **Age**: Chronological age is at least 6 years.
    - **Exclusion**: Not better explained by another mental disorder (e.g., Depression, Bipolar, Psychosis, APD, BPD) or substance use.

## Diagnosis (ICD-11 Criteria)
- **Code**: 6C73
- **Essential Features**:
    - **Repeated brief episodes** of verbal or physical aggression or destruction of property.
    - Represents a failure to control aggressive impulses.
    - **Intensity**: Grossly out of proportion to the provocation or stressor.
    - **Impulsivity**: Outbursts are impulsive and/or anger-based, not premeditated/instrumental.
    - **Impairment**: Significant impairment in personal, family, social, educational, or occupational functioning.
    - **Exclusion**: Not better explained by other disorders (e.g., ODD, Conduct Disorder, Bipolar).
- **Key Differences**:
    - **Consistency**: The definitions are highly consistent between DSM-5 and ICD-11, focusing on the impulsive, non-instrumental nature of the aggression and the disproportionality of the reaction.

## Clinical Context
- **Vs. Instrumental Aggression**: IED is "reactive" aggression (hot, emotional), separate from "proactive/instrumental" aggression (cold, goal-directed) seen in Conduct Disorder or Antisocial Personality Disorder.
- **Cycle**: Tension building -> Explosive Release -> Relief -> Remorse/Regret (often).
