# Conversion Disorder (Functional Neurological Symptom Disorder)

A condition where patients present with neurological symptoms (e.g., paralysis, seizures, blindness) that cannot be explained by neurological disease, but are real experiences for the patient.

## Diagnosis (DSM-5 Criteria)
- **Code**: 300.11
- **Essential Features**:
    - One or more symptoms of **altered voluntary motor or sensory function**.
    - Clinical findings provide evidence of **incompatibility** between the symptom and recognized neurological or medical conditions (e.g., "Hoover's sign" for leg weakness).
    - The symptom or deficit is not better explained by another medical or mental disorder.
    - Causes clinically significant distress or impairment.
- **Specifiers**:
    - With weakness or paralysis
    - With abnormal movement (e.g., tremor, dystonia)
    - With swallowing symptoms
    - With speech symptoms (e.g., dysphonia)
    - With attacks or seizures (Psychogenic Non-Epileptic Seizures - PNES)
    - With anesthesia or sensory loss

## Diagnosis (ICD-11 Criteria)
- **Name**: **Dissociative Neurological Symptom Disorder** (Code 6B60)
- **Also Known As**: Functional Neurological Symptom Disorder (FNSD).
- **Essential Features**:
    - Presentation of motor, sensory, or cognitive symptoms that imply a neurological disorder but are **incompatible** with known pathophysiology.
    - **Positive signs of incompatibility** are required for diagnosis (diagnosis is not just "exclusion of other diseases" but presence of specific internal inconsistency).
    - Not intentionally feigned (distinguishing it from Factitious Disorder/Malingering).
- **Key Differences**:
    - **Categorization**: ICD-11 places this under **Dissociative Disorders**, emphasizing the mechanism of dissociation (disconnection between brain function and consciousness). DSM-5 places it under **Somatic Symptom and Related Disorders**, emphasizing the physical presentation.
    - **Terminology**: The shift to "Functional Neurological Disorder" (FND) is gaining traction clinically to reduce stigma associated with "Conversion" (Freudian origin).

## Clinical Context
- **Mechanism**: Often viewed as a "software" problem of the brain rather than "hardware" (structural) damage.
- **Etiology**: Stress and trauma are common triggers, but psychological stressors are *not* strictly required for the diagnosis in DSM-5 (a change from DSM-IV).
- **Treatment**: Physical Therapy (specifically designed for FND) and CBT are effective.
