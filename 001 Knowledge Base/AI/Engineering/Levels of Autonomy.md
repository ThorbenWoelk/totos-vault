The layers of an onion of AI autonomy: 
LLMs < Agents < Multi-agents < Claws < Multi-claws ...

While industry leaders are constantly climbing these latter of abstractions, there's some limits to how high you can get in your daily work. 

1. **Token Limits**. Everyone online is acting as if they don't exist. For me, and most companies, they still do. A monthly $200 subscriptions per developer is not peanuts. I think navigating within a more restricted set boundaries can make you be more creative and open-minded. Try out different subscriptions, open-source models, and harnesses. Optimize your workflow. Allocate time for reflecting on your setup, manually review some code and deepen your understanding. Write something down. All of this improves your AI versatility and general knowledge. Maybe even go touch some grass once in a while.
2. **Good Context compounds**. Silicon valley's 10x engineers feed their agents with well-written codebases, clean coding guidelines, and best practice security setups. Depending on your starting point, you should expect inferior results from your agents which increases the need to review.  

By all means, spin up a multi-agent setup and let it run for 24h - if it brings the results you're hoping for. But don't let LinkedIn fool you that building a self-improving autoresearch crew with everlasting smart-compressed memory is what we all should be doing right now. 

It's still perfectly fine to stick to the **agent** level of autonomy and spend most of your time in a coding harness like Claude Code, Codex app or T3 Code. Try spawning N sessions in parallel, experiment with worktrees. You're learning a lot and doing just fine. 

Obviously, this is a time of rapid change. And burning out is easier than ever before. Pay attention to signs of AI psychosis. Don't FOMO too much or succumb to overconfidence. Keep experimenting.
